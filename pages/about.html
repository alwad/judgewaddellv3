Our Namesake

Ewell E. "Judge" Waddell

Coach. Teacher. Superintendent. The man who shaped generations of student-athletes at Highlands High School in Fort Thomas, Kentucky.

"'Judge' Waddell is a gentleman first and a coach second. He is the finest fellow that I have run into in the coaching profession. He is my best friend in Northern Kentucky."

— Tom Ellis, Covington Holmes Coach

Early Life & Education

Ewell E. "Judge" Waddell was born in 1907 in the small Western Kentucky town of Marion. He graduated from what is now Western Kentucky University after a career that was academically excellent and athletically extraordinary — playing the vital center position on a single wing offense. He was named a Little All-American in 1933.

Coming to Highlands

After a winning career at Ludlow High School, Judge Waddell came to Highlands in 1942. During his twelve years as a classroom teacher, he taught many subjects, including U.S. and World History, Economics, and Sociology.

He served as head football coach from 1942 through 1954. Those thirteen teams compiled a record of 91 and 37, won six conference titles, and claimed two Kentucky State Championships — this during a time when there were no school classifications, when Highlands was a relative David competing with Goliaths, and yet winning.

An intense rivalry existed in those days between Covington Holmes and Highlands. Nevertheless, Judge Waddell became a good friend of the Holmes coach, Tom Ellis. At the height of that rivalry, Ellis said: "'Judge' Waddell is a gentleman first and a coach second. He is the finest fellow that I have run into in the coaching profession. He is my best friend in Northern Kentucky."

Homer Rice and the "Total Person"

In 1954, Judge Waddell handed the reins to Homer Rice — who had played on one of his state championship teams — and moved on to lead the Fort Thomas School system as Superintendent. He successfully guided the district through the change-filled and challenging years of 1955 through 1967, and died suddenly after a twenty-five-year career in Fort Thomas. His sons Don, Bill, and Phill all played for Highlands.

Rice went on to a distinguished career at the high school, college, and professional levels, eventually serving as Athletic Director at Georgia Tech — where he is honored with buildings bearing his name and a statue alongside John Heisman and Bobby Dodd outside the stadium.

Rice became the author of several books and developed the Total Person concept, a sports leadership program now active in dozens of colleges and universities. That philosophy has its roots in what Judge Waddell taught on the fields of Fort Thomas, Kentucky.

Homer Rice, principal founder of this scholarship, died on June 10, 2024. His lifelong devotion to the Judge's memory and values is the spirit behind everything this fund does.

2016 Hall of Fame

In 2016, Coach Waddell was inducted into the Highlands High School Athletic Hall of Fame.

The Hilltopper, 1954

An article about Judge Waddell was published in the October 22, 1954 edition of the Hilltopper, the Highlands student newspaper.

The Highlands Tradition

Highlands High School in Fort Thomas has a rich athletic and academic tradition. Judge Waddell was central to building that culture — a place where excellence in the classroom and on the field were considered not competing priorities, but complementary ones.

The scholarship bearing his name seeks to continue that tradition by supporting student-athletes who embody what he believed in, and who intend to pass those values to the next generation as teachers and coaches.
